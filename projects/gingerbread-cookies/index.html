Chewy Gingerbread Cookies

A few years back I had the best, chewiest gingerbread cookie at a party. I didn’t think to ask for the recipe. Big mistake. Since then I’ve been on what can only be described as a quest to find a good, chewy gingerbread recipe.

I tried several recipes that claimed increasing the brown sugar to white sugar ratio would make the cookie chewier. This is lies. It does make the cookie gooier, but that is an entirely different thing than making the cookie chewier.

In a desperate attempt to avoid these gooey recipes, I googled “Gingerbread cake cookies” which, I know, is completely nonsensical. It involves three conflicting types of baked goods: bread, cake and cookies. But, what can I say, sometimes a girl wants it all. The search bore two promising results.

First, to my delight, a recipe with the exact title ‘Gingerbread cake cookie’ (google, you sweet, sweet siren). And second, a recipe called ‘Old-fashioned Williamsburg Gingerbread Cookies’. Not obviously a chewy recipe, but I figured I’d take a chance and trust that the colonial bakers of yore knew their way around a gingerbread cookie.

THE RECIPIES

Gingerbread Cake Cookies

For the cookies, makes about a dozen:

  • ¼ cup shortening
  • ½ cup sugar
  • 1 egg
  • ½ cup molasses
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda dissolved in ½ cup hot water
  • 2 cups flour
  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon ginger
  • ½ teaspoon nutmeg
  • ½ teaspoon ground cloves

For the frosting:

  • 1 cup powdered sugar
  • ¼ teaspoon vanilla
  • 2 tablespoons orange juice

Instructions: Cream together shortening and sugar, add egg and mollasses and beat well. Slowly combine dry ingredients int ot mixture. Spoon dough onto a cookie sheet. Bake for 8 minutes at 400 degrees. For the frosting, whisk together the ingredients until a glaze forms.

Old-Fashioned Williamsburg Gingerbread Cookies

Makes about 2 dozen cookies:

  • 1 cup white sugar
  • 2 tsp ginger
  • 1 tsp nutmeg
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 ½ tsp baking soda
  • 1 cup (2 sticks) butter, softened
  • ½ cup evaporated milk
  • 1 cup molasses
  • 4 cups all-purpose flour sifted

Instructions: Combine sugar, ginger, nutmeg, cinnamon, salt, and baking soda. Add the softened butter, evaporated milk, and mollasses. Mix until creamy. Gradually add the flour. Make a ball out of the dough, and roll out on a well floured surface. Cut out the cookies and place on a baking sheet. Bake for 10-12 minutes at 375 degrees.

Before you get started:

  • Do not attempt this baking while in bare feet or socks. If your kitchen is anything like mine, you will feel every crumb under your feet. Very unsettling.
  • Ask your Alexa to play ‘Cocktail Jazz’, I have found this to be calming when things get sticky

First up: Gingerbread Cake Cookies

To start this recipe you combine the sugar and wet ingredients and then fold in the dry ingredients (if you need it, here’s a refresher on how to fold in ingredients). Thus far the recipe is delightfully easy. I am feeling quite good about things. Pause for my husband to pop into the kitchen and remind me that I put his whiskey glasses away in the wrong place (heaven forbid). We continue. Now, I’ve finalized the dough. It is very sticky. I try to use my hands to create balls to place on the cooking sheet. This is not going to work. My hands are instantly covered in sticky dough, and I pivot around the kitchen for a second like a doctor who’s just sterilized his hands. I switch to a spoon. One spoon is not enough. This is double-spoon situation. Double-spoon does the trick and I am able to get nice globs onto the cookie sheet.

Now that the cookies are in the oven it’s on to the frosting. This frosting recipe is genius. Who knew powdered sugar, vanilla, and orange juice would congeal so nicely into a lovely glaze? I feel like it should be harder, but it’s not. This is the best kind of DIY. Store bought frosting has nothing on this recipe. Once the cookies are out I drizzle the frosting on them and that’s it. Cookies complete.

Next up: Old-fashioned Williamsburg Gingerbread Cookies

This recipe starts off a little shakier. It calls for softened butter. Softened butter is the worst. I’m sorry to be harsh, but it’s true. You will absolutely never remember to set out the butter ahead of time. And then, when you try to soften it via microwave it either melts too much or not enough. I’m convinced the concept of softened butter only exists so that the spirits of baker’s past can laugh at us from the afterlife. But, we press on.

Next up is the evaporated milk. This ingredient is showing the recipe’s age. Honestly, I had to google what this looked like to find it in the grocery store. And now I have ¾ of a can of evaporated milk (yes, it comes in a can) that will sit at the back of my refrigerator for eternity. The can also instructed me to shake well before opening. I’m skeptical. Nevertheless, we press on.

Despite these questionable ingredients (and the butter chunks caused by my no-so-softened butter) the dough actually comes together well. Unlike the Gingerbread Cake Cookies, this dough is meant to be rolled out. It rolls out nicely and I’m able to create my cookie cutouts. I lost my gingerbread man cookie cutter, so we do flowers. Deal with it. Cookies are in the oven and we are all set.

THE BOTTOM LINE

Gingerbread Cake Cookies
  • Effort: Small
  • Price: Requires investment in some expensive spices, but otherwise comparible with buying in store
  • Reward: Better than can usually be found in store
Old-Fashioned Williamsburg Gingerbread Cookies
  • Effort: Medium
  • Price: Requires investment in some expensive spices, but otherwise comparible with buying in store
  • Reward: Better than can usually be found in store

Overall winner: Old-Fashioned Williamsburg Gingerbread Cookie

Colonial bakers for the win. These cookies are a little more effort, but it's worth it for the traditional roll-out cookie. And these bad boys stay chewie days after baking.
